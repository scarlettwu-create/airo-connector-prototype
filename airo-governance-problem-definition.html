Why we're building AIRO agent governance

The shift

Everyone is racing to make agents easier to build. The bottleneck has moved. The hard part now is trusting an agent enough to let it act in real systems, and it's usually a whole crew of agents handing work to each other, which makes "who did what, and was it allowed" even harder to answer. That's a governance problem, and it's getting urgent fast.

Our own customers are blocking on it

Straight from our research:

  • Security teams are blocking Genie rollouts. "Security teams blocking Genie deployments" (Guardrails PRD, PMO-2078). "Enterprise security and legal block LLM adoption until this is solved" (AI/LLM Gateway PRD, PMO-2900).
  • They won't go to production without content/PII guardrails, real isolation, and an audit trail they trust.
  • No IT backdoor, keep their own audit logs. A payroll customer's hard line (Dovetail · Automation HQ Feedback Study).
  • Permissions are already painful to manage at scale (Dovetail · Permissions & Roles Foundational).
  • They're explicitly asking for trust and governance (Dovetail · Airo Trust).

Governance is the gate between our agents and production. It's a revenue gate, not polish.

The market says the same, louder

  • 68% of orgs can't tell an agent's action from a human's after the fact (TierZero).
  • 74% of agents are over-permissioned (TierZero). 51% have no owner for their AI identities, which already outnumber humans 45 to 1 (CSA).
  • Gartner expects 40% of enterprises to decommission agents by 2027 over governance gaps (Gartner), and "guardian agents" that watch other agents to reach 10 to 15% of the market by 2030 (Gartner).

It's already going wrong

The permission existed; the gate didn't. Replit's agent deleted a production database (The Register). A PocketOS agent wiped a database and every backup in nine seconds, all through "a permitted operation" (Eon). A poisoned GitHub issue made Devin escalate to remote code execution (Embrace The Red).

The layer we own

Identity (Entra, Okta, WorkOS), network (Kong), execution (Claude Code, Agentforce, AWS, Devin), and observability (Galileo, Arize) are all taken. The business-action layer is open, and it's ours. Three things only we have together:

  • stop a risky action before it happens (Rubrik only undoes it after),
  • understand what the action means, like a grant of admin or a finance export (Kong only sees traffic),
  • tie it back to a person, even when a whole crew of agents touched it.

Why this is our breakthrough

Two wins at once: unblock deployment by giving security what they're blocking on, and own the layer no one else does by governing the whole run, tied to a person, in the path where the action actually happens.

One line

We don't just help you build agents. We make them safe enough to actually put into production, and that's what unblocks the whole thing.
